Definition of Temperature

Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of particles in a substance, indicating how hot or cold it is. It is commonly measured in degrees Celsius (°C), Fahrenheit (°F), or Kelvin (K).

Common formulas for calculating Temperature include:

  • Celsius to Fahrenheit: F = 9/5C + 32
  • Fahrenheit to Celsius: C = 5/9(F - 32)
  • Celsius to Kelvin: K = C + 273.15
  • Kelvin to Celsius: C = K − 273.15

Units of Temperature

Celsius (°C):

A scale used widely around the world, based on the freezing point of water (0°C) and the boiling point of water (100°C) at standard atmospheric pressure.

Fahrenheit (°F):

Primarily used in the United States, where water freezes at 32°F and boils at 212°F at standard atmospheric pressure.

Kelvin (K):

The SI unit for temperature is Kelvin (K), used in scientific contexts, starting at absolute zero (0 K), the point where all particle motion ceases. The Kelvin scale shares the same incremental value as Celsius but does not use the degree symbol. Absolute zero is equivalent to -273.15°C.

Rankine (°R):

The Rankine scale (°R) is an absolute temperature scale used in engineering, based on Fahrenheit increments. Absolute zero is 0°R, equivalent to -459.67°F.

Frequently Asked Questions

To convert Celsius to Fahrenheit, use the formula: Fahrenheit = (Celsius × 9/5) + 32.
Example:
25°C = (25 × 9/5) + 32 = 77°F.

To convert Fahrenheit to Celsius, use the formula: Celsius = (Fahrenheit - 32) × 5/9.
Example:
77°F = (77 - 32) × 5/9 = 25°C.

To convert Kelvin to Celsius, use the formula: Celsius = Kelvin - 273.15.
Example:
300K = 300 - 273.15 = 26.85°C.

The freezing point of water is: 0°C (Celsius), 32°F (Fahrenheit), 273.15K (Kelvin).

Celsius and Fahrenheit are the same at -40 degrees.
Formula:
Set F = C in the formula F = (9/5 × C) + 32 and solve to find C = -40.
